## Design Context

### Users
Mandarin language learners who are serious about their practice — not casual app users, but people who want a focused, repeatable tool for building real fluency. They use Kuai the way a runner uses a stopwatch: with discipline and without distraction. Many will be intermediate learners who have moved past vocabulary apps and want a typing interface that respects their intelligence and commitment. They open the app, enter the flow state, and type.

### Brand Personality
**Zen, Sharp, Timeless.**

Kuai is a modern tool that respects its roots. It is not a dusty museum piece, and it is not a cheerful language app. It sits closer to a well-worn calligraphy practice book than a mobile game — precise, quiet, and deeply considered. The sharpness comes from the interaction itself: the cadence of Pinyin input, the rhythm of tones. The UI should never distract from that rhythm.

### Aesthetic Direction
**Right references:** Monkeytype (obsessive typographic clarity, stat-focus, no fluff), Okami (ink wash / sumi-e visual language), Muji (restraint as a design decision, negative space as an element).

**Wrong references:** Duolingo and its successors — no rounded bubbly buttons, no neon gradients, no frantic micro-animations, no gamification clichés. If it looks like a standard mobile game, the "Art of Typing" is lost.

**Theme:** Light mode only. Base is warm off-white (`#f8f7f4`) — soft enough to prevent eye strain during long sessions without the "buzziness" of pure white. Expand through **texture and depth**, not color. Think paper varieties (Xuan paper, silk) and ink density as future aesthetic dimensions.

**Color discipline:** The palette is strictly monochrome except in battle mode. Any future color introduction must come from traditional Chinese pigments — Cinnabar red, Jade green — used as rare "seals" or stamps, never as UI accents. The current red/green in battle mode should be held to this standard.

**Typography:** Three-font system is load-bearing. Ma Shan Zheng for Chinese calligraphic display, Protest Revolution for branded headlines, Spectral for body and UI. Do not introduce additional typefaces.

### Design Principles

1. **The keyboard is the brush.** Every interaction should feel like a deliberate stroke — no filler animations, no idle motion. What moves on screen should mean something.

2. **Restraint is the brand.** Adding more is almost always wrong. Expand through texture, weight, and spacing before reaching for new colors, fonts, or elements. Less is not a compromise; it is the point.

3. **Flow state is sacred.** The UI exists to disappear during active typing. No alerts, badges, banners, or decorations should interrupt the meditative grind of a session. Save feedback for natural pause points.

4. **Sharp, not sterile.** Monochrome does not mean clinical. Ink textures, paper warmth, and brush-stroke assets are what separate Kuai from a generic productivity tool. Protect them. Do not flatten them in the name of "clean."

5. **Accessibility through shape, not color.** Since color is used sparingly, success/failure states must be communicated through shape, texture, or motion — not color alone. Colorblind users must never be disadvantaged. Provide a "Reduced Ink" mode for users with vestibular sensitivities that settles brush-stroke and ink-bleed animations without sacrificing the overall aesthetic character.
